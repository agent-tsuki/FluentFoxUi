## Concept 01: The Potential Form (可能形)
In Chapter 17, you learned `koto ga dekimasu`. Now, you will learn how to conjugate the verb itself into the **Potential Form**.

### Group I:
Change the **~u** sound (Dictionary Form) to the **~e** sound and add **る**.
* 行く (iku) → **行ける** (ikeru - can go)
* 飲む (nomu) → **飲める** (nomeru - can drink)

### Group II:
Replace **〜る** with **〜られる**.
* 食べる (taberu) → **食べられる** (taberareru - can eat)
* 見る (miru) → **見られる** (mirareru - can see)

### Group III (Irregular):
* する (suru) → **できる** (dekiru - can do)
* 来る (kuru) → **来られる** (korareru - can come)



---

## Concept 02: Particle Change with Potential
When a verb is changed into the potential form, the object particle **を** usually changes to **が**. Other particles like **に, へ, と** do not change.

* **日本語（にほんご）を 話（はな）します。** (I speak Japanese.)
* **日本語 が 話せます。** (I **can** speak Japanese.)

---

## Concept 03: Visible vs. Audible (見える / 聞こえる)
These are special potential-like verbs that describe natural perception rather than a conscious "ability."

1.  **見える (mieru):** To be in sight / visible (without effort).
    * 窓（まど）から 山（やま）が **見えます**。 (The mountain is visible from the window.)
2.  **聞（き）こえる (kikoeru):** To be audible / can hear.
    * 波（なみ）の 音（おと）が **聞こえます**。 (I can hear the sound of the waves.)

---

## Concept 04: "Only" with Potential (しか...ない)
Combining the potential form with **しか**, you can express that you can *only* do one specific thing.

* **ひらがな しか 書（か）けません。**
    * *Hiragana shika kakemasen.*
    * I can only write Hiragana (cannot write Kanji).

---

## Concept 05: Completed Action (〜てしまいました)
This pattern expresses two things: either an action is **completely finished** or an action happened that the speaker **regrets**.

* **宿題（しゅくだい）を 全部（ぜんぶ） やってしまいました。**
    * (I finished all my homework completely.)
* **パスポートを なくしてしまいました。**
    * (I [unfortunately] lost my passport.)



---

## Sidebar: Reference & Nuance

### Potential vs. Dekimasu
While `koto ga dekimasu` is perfectly fine, the **Potential Form** is much more common in daily speech and on the JLPT N4 exam. It makes your Japanese sound much more natural.


### Pro-Tip: Software States
In your government job portal project, you could use **〜てしまいました** for error states (e.g., "The session has expired") and **可能形 (Potential)** for user permissions (e.g., "The user **can** apply for this job").
